# Why Paper Field Notes Are Leaking Billable Hours for EHS Consulting Firms

**Short answer:** Every hour a hygienist spends transcribing handwritten notes into a formal report is an hour that can't be billed to a client, and every forgotten data point that forces a return site visit is a trip that eats margin instead of adding it.[1] For small EHS and IH consulting firms, this isn't an efficiency nice-to-have — it's a direct line to the bottom line.

## Where do billable hours actually leak?

Not usually where firm owners assume. The site visit itself is billable time well spent. The leaks happen downstream, in unbilled administrative work:

- **Transcription** — converting a clipboard or notebook into a structured, client-ready document
- **Data reconstruction** — realizing at write-up that a location, reading, or PPE note wasn't captured clearly and having to reconstruct it from memory
- **Re-testing trips** — the costliest leak of all: driving back to a client's facility because a single forgotten data point invalidated part of a sample or observation[1]
- **Review cycles** — a senior reviewer catching inconsistent formatting or missing fields, sending the report back for rework

None of that is billable to the client. All of it is real cost to the firm.

## Why does one missing field cost more than it looks like?

Because the downstream cost isn't the five minutes it takes to notice the gap — it's everything that follows.

- If the missing data invalidates a sample, the fix often requires a **second site visit**, which means travel time, scheduling friction, and sometimes a client relationship strained by the delay.
- If it doesn't invalidate the sample but does weaken the report, it becomes a **judgment call** the reviewing hygienist has to document and defend later.
- Either way, the firm absorbs the cost. The client doesn't pay extra for a redo caused by an incomplete clipboard entry.

## What does the math look like at firm scale?

For a lean 10–40 person consultancy running on billable-hour margins, this compounds fast:

- A CIH who spends even one hour a day on transcription and data reconstruction is losing roughly 20% of a standard workday to unbillable admin.
- Multiply that across every hygienist on staff, every week, and the "cost of paper" becomes a real line item — even though it never appears on an invoice.
- A single avoided re-test trip can be worth more than the cost of switching tools for the entire quarter.
