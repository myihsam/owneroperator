# Electronic Chain of Custody: Closing the Gap Between Field Sample and Lab Result

**Short answer:** A chain of custody exists to prove a sample was in the possession of, or secured by, a responsible person from the moment of collection through final analysis or disposal.[1] It's a simple concept that breaks easily on paper — a missing signature, an illegible field, a form that never made it into the sample cooler. For consultancies working with AIHA-accredited laboratories, a broken chain of custody can undermine an otherwise perfectly valid sample.

## What is chain of custody, and why does it matter for IH samples?

Chain of custody documentation tracks a sample's possession continuously, from field collection through lab receipt, analysis, and eventual disposal.[1] It exists to answer one question if a result is ever challenged: can you prove nothing happened to this sample that would compromise the result?

- AIHA-accredited laboratories operate under quality systems that trace back to ISO/IEC 17025, the international standard for testing and calibration laboratories.[2][3]
- Accredited labs maintain rigorous documentation control over their own procedures and analyst training — but that rigor only protects a sample once it arrives.[4]
- Everything that happens *before* the sample reaches the lab — labeling, sealing, field-to-lab handoff — is the consultancy's responsibility to document.

## Where does chain of custody typically break down?

Almost always on the field side, not the lab side:

- **Illegible or incomplete forms** — required fields (sample ID, date, collector, analysis requested) filled in inconsistently across a busy sampling day
- **Lost or damaged paper forms** — a form left in a vehicle, water-damaged in the field, or simply misplaced between collection and shipment
- **Handoff gaps** — no clear record of who had custody of the sample between the collection point and the shipping courier
- **Mismatched sample IDs** — a labeling error that decouples the physical sample from its documented chain

Any one of these can force a lab to flag or reject a sample, or force a consultancy to explain a documentation gap during a legal or regulatory challenge — regardless of how clean the underlying data actually is.

## What does a complete chain of custody record need?

At minimum, for each sample:

- Unique sample ID matching the physical sample label
- Date, time, and location of collection
- Analysis requested and applicable method
- Name of the person who collected the sample
- Signature and timestamp at every custody transfer point
- Shipping/receipt confirmation from the laboratory

## Frequently asked questions

**What is chain of custody in industrial hygiene sampling?**
Documentation that tracks a sample's possession continuously from field collection through laboratory analysis, proving it wasn't compromised along the way.

**Where does chain of custody most often break down?**
On the field side — illegible or incomplete paper forms, lost documentation, or unclear handoff records between collection and shipment.

**Does an accredited lab's quality system cover chain of custody before the sample arrives?**
No. Lab accreditation covers the laboratory's own procedures and analysis; documentation before the sample reaches the lab is the collecting consultancy's responsibility.
